The World After World War II

A new era begins in 1945. Two superpowers. A divided world. The birth of the Cold War.

Overview

A new kind of war, without a battlefield.

```

In 1945, World War II ended. More than 60 million people had died. Cities lay in ruins. Europe was exhausted. But a new kind of conflict was already beginning.

Two countries came out of the war much stronger than everyone else: the United States and the Soviet Union. We call them superpowers. They had very different ideas about how people should live, how governments should work, and who should own businesses.

This disagreement became the Cold War. It was called "cold" because the two superpowers never fought each other directly. Instead, they fought with money, spies, ideas, and the threat of nuclear weapons. And the world was divided in two.

At the same time, in Asia, a massive civil war was ending in China. Its result would change the balance of power for the next century.

Timeline

Twenty years that remade the world.

Click or tap any event to learn more. The story starts before World War II ends, because the seeds of the Cold War were already growing.

```
1927

Chinese Civil War begins

Nationalists (led by Chiang Kai-shek) fight against Communists (led by Mao Zedong) for control of China.

1937

Japan invades China

Chinese Nationalists and Communists pause their fighting and unite against the Japanese invasion.

February 1945

Yalta Conference

Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin meet to plan the post-war world. They divide Germany into four zones.

May 1945

Germany surrenders

World War II ends in Europe. Soviet troops occupy Eastern Europe; American and British troops hold the West.

August 1945

Hiroshima & Nagasaki

The U.S. drops atomic bombs on two Japanese cities. Japan surrenders. The nuclear age begins.

October 1945

United Nations founded

51 countries sign the UN Charter. Its goal: prevent another world war and protect human rights.

March 1946

"Iron Curtain" speech

Winston Churchill warns that an "Iron Curtain" has fallen across Europe, dividing free nations from Soviet-controlled ones.

March 1947

Truman Doctrine

President Truman promises U.S. support to any country fighting against communism. The Cold War strategy is set.

June 1947

Marshall Plan

The U.S. sends $13 billion to help rebuild Western Europe — and keep it out of Soviet hands.

1948 – 1949

Berlin Airlift

The Soviets block West Berlin. The U.S. and Britain fly food and fuel to the city for nearly a year. Stalin backs down.

April 1949

NATO is formed

The U.S., Canada, and ten Western European nations promise to defend each other against Soviet attack.

October 1949

Mao wins in China

The Chinese Communists defeat the Nationalists. Mao Zedong proclaims the People's Republic of China.

May 1955

Warsaw Pact

The Soviet Union and its Eastern European allies form their own military alliance. Europe is officially two armed camps.

```

Communism vs. Capitalism

Two answers to one big question.

Who should own factories, farms, and businesses? Who decides how much workers get paid? The answers split the 20th-century world in half.

```
East

Communism

Soviet Union, China (after 1949), Cuba, North Korea

  • The government owns everything.Factories, farms, and land belong to the state, not private people.
  • One political party.Only the Communist Party is allowed. No free elections.
  • Planned economy.The government decides what is made, how much, and the price.
  • Goal: total equality.No rich or poor. In theory. In reality, leaders lived well while people suffered.
  • Founder of the idea:Karl Marx (German philosopher, 1818–1883).
West

Capitalism

United States, United Kingdom, France, West Germany, Japan

  • Private people own businesses.You can start a company, buy property, and keep the profit.
  • Democracy and free elections.People vote for their leaders. Many political parties compete.
  • Free market.Supply and demand set prices. The government mostly stays out of business decisions.
  • Goal: freedom and opportunity.People can become wealthy through work or ideas. But inequality can be high.
  • Rooted in the ideas of:Adam Smith (Scottish economist, 1723–1790).
```
Chinese Civil War

A civil war, paused by a world war.

The conflict between Chinese Nationalists and Communists ran for 22 years. It stopped for eight of those years — only so both sides could fight Japan. When WWII ended, they picked up right where they left off.

```
Phase 01
1927–1937

The first fight

Nationalists (KMT) under Chiang Kai-shek try to crush the Communists. Mao Zedong leads the survivors on the Long March — a 6,000-mile retreat.

Phase 02
1937–1945

Truce against Japan

Japan invades China. Nationalists and Communists stop fighting each other to defend their country. Both grow stronger during WWII.

Phase 03
1945–1949

Civil war returns

Japan surrenders. The truce is over. Mao's Communists, supported by peasants, slowly push Chiang's Nationalists out of mainland China.

Phase 04
October 1949

Mao wins

Mao proclaims the People's Republic of China. Chiang and the Nationalists escape to the island of Taiwan. The world's most populous country is now communist.

"The Chinese people have stood up."

— Mao Zedong, proclaiming the People's Republic · October 1, 1949
```

```

A nuclear weapon with enormous power. The U.S. was the first country to build one. Two atomic bombs were used on Japan in August 1945. They killed more than 150,000 people and ended World War II. After the war, both superpowers raced to build more.

In 1948, the Soviets blocked all roads into West Berlin to starve it. For almost a year, the U.S. and Britain flew food, coal, and medicine into the city every few minutes. It was a major Cold War victory without a single shot fired.

An economic system where private people (not the government) own businesses and property. Prices are set by supply and demand. People can become rich through work, ideas, or investment. The United States is the classic example.

Leader of the Chinese Nationalists (KMT). He fought Japan during WWII and Mao's Communists before and after. He lost the civil war in 1949 and fled to the island of Taiwan, where he ran a separate government until his death in 1975.

The long conflict between the United States and the Soviet Union from 1945 to 1991. They did not fight each other with soldiers. They competed with money, spies, technology, sports, and the constant threat of nuclear war.

An economic and political system in which the government owns all businesses and land. The goal, in theory, is equality — no rich, no poor. In practice, communist countries like the Soviet Union became dictatorships ruled by a single party.

The main U.S. strategy in the Cold War. The idea was simple: stop communism from spreading to any new country. The Truman Doctrine, the Marshall Plan, and NATO were all tools of containment.

The process by which European colonies in Asia and Africa became independent countries after WWII. Britain, France, and other powers could no longer afford to hold their empires. Dozens of new nations were born between 1945 and 1975.

The invisible line that divided Europe during the Cold War. West of the line: free, democratic, capitalist nations. East of the line: communist countries controlled by the Soviet Union. Winston Churchill named it in a famous 1946 speech.

Leader of the Chinese Communist Party. He won the Chinese Civil War in 1949 and founded the People's Republic of China. He ruled until his death in 1976. His policies caused enormous progress — and enormous suffering.

A U.S. program (1948–1951) that sent about $13 billion to help Western European countries rebuild after WWII. The goal was both kind and strategic: help allies recover, and keep them out of Soviet hands.

North Atlantic Treaty Organization. A military alliance created in 1949 by the U.S., Canada, and Western European countries. If one member is attacked, all members promise to defend it. Still exists today with 32 members.

A phrase describing the way the world was organized after 1945. The old European empires were weak. The U.S. and Soviet Union became the dominant powers. New institutions like the UN, the IMF, and the World Bank were built to manage a global system.

The official name of communist China, declared by Mao Zedong on October 1, 1949. It replaced the Nationalist government on the Chinese mainland. Today it is the most populous country on Earth and a global superpower.

A country with so much military, economic, and cultural power that it shapes events across the globe. After WWII, there were only two: the United States and the Soviet Union.

A 1947 speech by U.S. President Harry Truman. He promised American support to any country fighting against communism. This marked the official beginning of containment and the Cold War.

An international organization founded in October 1945 with 51 countries. Its goal: prevent another world war and protect human rights. Today almost every country on Earth is a member — 193 in total.

A military alliance created in 1955 by the Soviet Union and its Eastern European communist allies. It was the communist response to NATO. The pact ended in 1991 when the Soviet Union collapsed.

A meeting in February 1945 between Franklin Roosevelt (U.S.), Winston Churchill (UK), and Joseph Stalin (USSR). They agreed on how to divide Germany and organize post-war Europe. It is often seen as the beginning of the Cold War division.
