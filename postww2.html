Overview

A world rebuilt, then divided.

World War II ended in 1945, but peace did not mean calm. Two superpowers rose, Europe split, nuclear fear spread, and China moved toward a communist revolution.

What students should understand

These ideas keep showing up through the rest of the unit, so this section gives the big picture first.

01
Two powers dominated the postwar world. The United States and Soviet Union came out of WWII stronger than everyone else and began competing for influence.
02
The Cold War shaped global politics. The superpowers rarely fought directly, but they battled through alliances, aid, weapons, propaganda, and proxy wars.
03
China changed the balance of power. When Mao and the Communists won in 1949, the communist world grew dramatically in size and importance.

Key Ideas

Big concepts, student-friendly language.

These cards keep the reading light, but the ideas strong. Each one is written to help students remember the core meaning fast.

01

Cold War

A long rivalry between the United States and Soviet Union. They avoided direct war, but competed through influence, weapons, money, and fear.

Think: pressure without full battle.
02

Iron Curtain

A phrase for the division between Western Europe and Eastern Europe under Soviet influence after World War II.

Think: one continent, two systems.
03

Containment

The main U.S. strategy to prevent communism from spreading into more countries.

Think: stop expansion before it grows.
Communism vs. Capitalism

Two systems. Two visions for society.

Students often remember this part best when it is simple and visual, so the compare section is stripped down to the biggest differences.

East

Communism

Soviet Union, China after 1949, Cuba, North Korea

  • State ownershipFactories, farms, and major industries belong to the government.
  • One-party rulePolitical power is usually controlled by one communist party.
  • Planned economyThe government decides what is produced and how resources are used.
  • Goal of equalityCommunism claimed to remove class divisions, though real life often looked very different.
West

Capitalism

United States, United Kingdom, France, West Germany, Japan

  • Private ownershipIndividuals and companies can own property and businesses.
  • Political competitionDemocracies usually include elections and multiple parties.
  • Market economyPrices are shaped by supply, demand, and consumer choice.
  • Goal of opportunityCapitalism emphasizes freedom, profit, and innovation, but can also create inequality.
Chinese Civil War

A revolution shaped by war, survival, and power.

The Chinese Civil War is easier to understand when students see it as a sequence: fight, pause, return, victory.

Phase 1
1927-1937

The first struggle

Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists attack the Communists. Mao survives and builds support in the countryside.

Phase 2
1937-1945

Pause for Japan

Japan invades China, and both sides temporarily stop fighting each other to resist the invasion.

Phase 3
1945-1949

War resumes

After WWII, the truce ends. The Communists gain support and begin winning more territory.

Phase 4
1949

Mao takes power

Mao proclaims the People's Republic of China while Chiang retreats to Taiwan.

"The Chinese people have stood up."
Mao Zedong, October 1, 1949

Flash Glossary

Fast review terms for student memory.

This section keeps the vocabulary short and clear so students can review quickly before discussion or homework.

Superpower

A country strong enough to shape world events through military, economic, and political power.

Marshall Plan

A U.S. program that sent billions of dollars to help rebuild Western Europe after WWII.

NATO

A military alliance formed in 1949 to defend Western countries against possible Soviet attack.
