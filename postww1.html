1919 – 1939

From War
to War

World War I ended in 1918. But the peace was not really peace. The decisions made after the war created anger, poverty, and fear — and new, dangerous leaders used those feelings to take power. This is the story of how the world moved from one war to the next.

▼
Section 1

The Treaty of Versailles & the War Guilt Clause

After World War I, the winning countries (the Allies) forced Germany to sign the Treaty of Versailles in 1919. Article 231 — the War Guilt Clause — said Germany was completely responsible for the war. This was not just words. It came with real punishments that crushed Germany.

💡 Think of it this way: Imagine you and four friends get into a fight. Afterward, everyone blames only YOU. You have to pay for all the damage. You lose your car, your room, and you can only carry a small bag. That is what happened to Germany.

💰

Reparations

Germany had to pay 33 billion dollars (in 1919 money — that is hundreds of billions today). Germany did not have enough money. The government started printing more and more money, which destroyed the value of their currency. This is called hyperinflation.

🗺️

Lost Territory

Germany lost 13% of its land and 10% of its people. Alsace-Lorraine went back to France. Parts of eastern Germany went to the new country of Poland. Germany also lost all its colonies in Africa and Asia. Millions of Germans now lived in other countries.
Click to learn more ▸
⚔️

Military Limits

Germany's army was cut to only 100,000 soldiers. No air force. No submarines. Only 6 small warships. The Rhineland (the border area with France) had to be demilitarized — no soldiers allowed. Germany went from one of the strongest militaries in the world to almost nothing.
Click to learn more ▸
😤

National Humiliation

Germans called the treaty a "Diktat" — a dictated peace. They had no choice. The German people felt deep shame and anger. Many believed the treaty was unfair. This humiliation became fuel for extremist leaders like Hitler, who promised to tear up the treaty and restore German pride.

Section 2

Hyperinflation: When Money Becomes Worthless

To pay the reparations, the German government printed enormous amounts of money. But printing more money does not create more wealth — it just makes each bill worth less. Prices went up so fast that workers had to spend their pay the same day, before it became worthless.

🍞 Price of One Loaf of Bread in Germany
(in German Marks)
```
1918
0.5 Mark
Normal price — a worker could buy bread easily

200 BILLION marks for one loaf! People burned money for heat because it was worth less than wood.
```

📌 Why does this matter? When people lose their savings, their jobs, and cannot feed their families, they become desperate. Desperate people look for someone to blame — and for a strong leader who promises to fix everything. This is exactly what happened in Germany.

☭ Communism

Who owns things?
The government owns ALL property — factories, farms, land, businesses. No private ownership. The idea is that everything belongs to everyone equally.
Who has power?
In theory: the workers. In practice: one political party (the Communist Party) controls everything. No other parties allowed. No real elections.
The goal
A classless society — no rich, no poor. Everyone is supposed to have the same amount. Workers are valued above all.
The problem
In real life, communist governments often became dictatorships. The leaders lived in luxury while ordinary people suffered. Anyone who disagreed was punished or killed.
Real World Example

The Soviet Union (Russia) under Lenin and then Stalin. After the Russian Revolution in 1917, the Bolsheviks created the world's first communist state. Stalin took power in the late 1920s and turned the USSR into one of the most brutal dictatorships in history.

🤝 Socialism

Who owns things?
The government or the community owns major industries (like energy, healthcare, transportation). But people can still own personal property — their house, car, small businesses.
How is it different from communism?
Socialism can exist in a democracy. People still vote. There can be multiple political parties. Communism usually requires revolution and one-party rule. Think of socialism as the middle ground.
Think of it this way

If your school cafeteria gave free lunch to every student, paid for by taxes — that is a socialist idea. Everyone chips in so everyone is fed. You still own your backpack, your phone, your clothes. But the big, important things are shared.

💼 Capitalism

Who owns things?
Private individuals and companies. You can start a business, make a product, and keep the profits. The government stays mostly out of the economy.
How does it work?
Competition and supply and demand. If you make a good product, people buy it and you succeed. If not, you fail. The market — not the government — decides what gets made and what things cost.
Real World Example

The United States. Anyone can try to start a business. There are rich people and poor people. The government does not decide your salary or where you work. The strength: freedom and opportunity. The risk: inequality — some people become extremely rich while others stay poor.

✊ Fascism

What is it?
An extreme right-wing system where ONE leader has total power. The nation and its glory come above everything — above individual rights, above truth, above law. Fascism uses violence, propaganda, and fear to control people.
Key features
Aggressive nationalism (our nation is superior). Military power is glorified. An enemy group is blamed for all problems (Jews in Germany, for example). No free press. No opposition allowed. The leader is treated like a god.
How to picture fascism

Imagine a school where the principal has ALL the power. No student council. No parent meetings. You cannot question any rule. You must wear the school uniform, chant the school motto, and report any student who complains. Students from one neighborhood are told they are better than everyone else. Students from another neighborhood are punished and expelled just for existing. That is fascism — but with a whole country.

🏴 Nationalism

What is it?
A deep belief that your nation is special and should be independent and powerful. At its best, nationalism can unite people and build pride. At its worst, it leads to hatred of other countries and groups, and the belief that your people are naturally better than others.
Nationalism after WWI
Remember: nationalism helped START World War I. After the war, it got WORSE, not better. Germans felt humiliated. Italians felt cheated (they fought on the winning side but did not get the territory they were promised). Japanese leaders wanted a larger empire. These angry nationalisms all fed the rise of dictators.
Section 4

Nationalism After WWI: From Pride to Rage

During WWI, nationalism meant: "My country is great. I will fight for it." After WWI, nationalism turned darker. It became: "My country was wronged. Someone must pay." This shift is one of the most important reasons World War II happened.

🇩🇪

Germany

Humiliated by the Treaty of Versailles. Forced to accept total blame. Lost territory, military, and wealth. Germans wanted revenge and a leader who would restore their honor. Hitler promised exactly that.

Italy

Italy fought on the WINNING side, but the Allies did not give Italy the territory they promised. Italians felt betrayed. The economy collapsed. Workers went on strike. People were afraid of a communist revolution. Mussolini took advantage of this chaos to seize power.

Soviet Union (Russia)

Russia had its own revolution DURING WWI (1917). The Tsar was overthrown and the Bolsheviks (communists) took over. After years of civil war, Stalin rose to power in the late 1920s. He used extreme nationalism — Soviet pride — alongside communist ideology to build a totalitarian state.

Section 5

Totalitarianism: Total Control Over Everything

A totalitarian government controls EVERY part of life — not just laws and the economy, but what you think, say, read, believe, and even who your friends are. It is the most extreme form of dictatorship. Both fascist and communist governments in this period became totalitarian. Click each piece of the puzzle below.

📢

Propaganda

The government controls all information

Newspapers, radio, movies, posters, art — everything says the same message: the leader is great, the nation is strong, the enemy is evil. No other viewpoints are allowed. Children learn propaganda in school. The goal: make people believe the government's version of reality.
👁️

Secret Police

Spying on your own people

The Gestapo (Germany), the NKVD (Soviet Union), the OVRA (Italy). These secret police forces watched citizens, listened to conversations, and arrested anyone who criticized the government. Even children were encouraged to report their own parents.
🗳️

One-Party Rule

No elections, no choices

Only ONE political party is legal. No opposition. No debates. The leader of the party IS the leader of the country. In Germany it was the Nazi Party. In Italy, the Fascist Party. In the USSR, the Communist Party. If you joined another political group, you could be jailed or killed.
🎓

Control of Education

Schools teach loyalty to the state

Textbooks were rewritten to praise the leader. History was changed. Science that disagreed with the government's beliefs was banned. Youth groups (like the Hitler Youth in Germany or the Young Pioneers in the USSR) trained children to be loyal soldiers and citizens from a young age.
⛓️

Terror & Violence

Fear is the main tool of control

Concentration camps. Labor camps (gulags). Public executions. Mass purges. People disappeared in the middle of the night. The message was clear: obey, or this will happen to you and your family.
🏭

Economic Control

The government decides what to produce

In the USSR, Five-Year Plans told every factory what to make and how much. In Nazi Germany, the economy was organized around preparing for war. Workers had limited rights. Strikes were illegal. The economy served the state — not the people.
Section 6

Fascism vs. Stalin's Communism: Same Evil, Different Excuse

Both fascism and Stalin's communism were totalitarian — total control over everything. Both used violence, propaganda, and fear. But they had very different IDEAS about why they should have power. Think of two bullies in school — they both hurt people, but they give different reasons for it.

Fascism (Hitler / Mussolini) Stalin's Communism (USSR)
Big Idea The NATION is everything. Our race / people are superior. The WORKERS should control everything. End class inequality.
Who Is the Enemy? Other races, other nations, immigrants, minorities (especially Jewish people in Germany) The rich (bourgeoisie), landowners, capitalists, and anyone the Party calls a "class enemy"
Economy Private businesses can exist, but they serve the state. The government tells businesses what to produce. NO private ownership. The government owns ALL factories, farms, land. Central planning (Five-Year Plans).
Leadership A single dictator with a cult of personality. The leader IS the nation. A single dictator ruling through the Communist Party. Stalin eliminated anyone who could be a rival.
Violence Concentration camps, Holocaust, racial persecution, military aggression Gulags (forced labor camps), Great Purge (killed perceived enemies), forced famine in Ukraine (Holodomor)
What They Said vs. What They Did Said: "We will make our nation great again." Did: Started the deadliest war in history. Said: "We will create equality for all workers." Did: Created a system where millions starved and were enslaved.
Section 7

The Rise of the Dictators

These three men took power in countries that were suffering. They promised solutions. They delivered destruction. Click each card to learn how they came to power.

🇮🇹

Benito Mussolini

Italy — Fascist Dictator (1922–1943)
How he came to power
Italy was in economic chaos after WWI. Mussolini created the Fascist Party and organized paramilitary squads called "Blackshirts" who used violence against opponents. In 1922, he threatened to march on Rome with his followers. The King, afraid of civil war, made Mussolini prime minister. He then eliminated all opposition and declared himself "Il Duce" (The Leader).
Key idea
Mussolini was the FIRST fascist dictator. Hitler admired him and copied many of his tactics.
```
🇩🇪

Adolf Hitler

Germany — Nazi Dictator (1933–1945)
How he came to power
Hitler used Germany's anger over the Treaty of Versailles and the economic disaster of hyperinflation and the Great Depression. He was a powerful public speaker who blamed Jews, communists, and the Weimar Republic for Germany's problems. His Nazi Party won seats in parliament. In 1933 he was appointed Chancellor (prime minister). He then used an emergency law to give himself total power and became Führer (Leader).
Key idea
Hitler did not take power by force alone. He was appointed legally first, THEN destroyed democracy from the inside.
🇷🇺

Joseph Stalin

Soviet Union — Communist Dictator (late 1920s–1953)
How he came to power
After the Russian Revolution of 1917, Lenin led the new communist state. When Lenin died in 1924, there was a power struggle. Stalin was not the most popular or the smartest leader. But he was the most ruthless politician. He outmaneuvered his rivals (especially Trotsky), took control of the Communist Party, and by the late 1920s had total power. He then eliminated ANYONE who could challenge him.
Key idea
Stalin used communism as a tool for his own power. The communist ideal of equality was never the real goal — control was.
```
Section 8

The Chain: How WWI Led to WWII

History is a chain of causes and effects. Each event connects to the next. Follow this chain from the end of WWI to the beginning of WWII.

1918 — WWI Ends

Millions dead. Europe is destroyed. People want someone to blame.

↓

1919 — Treaty of Versailles

Germany is blamed for everything. Forced to pay billions. Loses territory and military. The German people feel humiliated and angry.

↓

1920s — Economic Disaster

Hyperinflation destroys German savings. Bread costs billions of marks. The middle class is wiped out. Italy's economy also collapses. Then comes the Great Depression (1929), hitting the whole world.

↓

1920s–1930s — Rise of Extremism

Desperate people turn to extreme leaders. Mussolini takes Italy (1922). Stalin consolidates the USSR (late 1920s). Hitler takes Germany (1933). Each promises strength, order, and national glory.

↓

1930s — Aggression & Appeasement

Hitler rebuilds the military (breaking the Treaty). Takes back the Rhineland, then Austria, then Czechoslovakia. Britain and France try to avoid war by giving Hitler what he wants (appeasement). It does not work.

↓

1939 — World War II Begins

Hitler invades Poland on September 1, 1939. Britain and France declare war. The chain that started with the Treaty of Versailles has reached its terrible conclusion.
